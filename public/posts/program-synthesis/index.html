    An Article on Program Synthesis: A Deep Learning Perspective

    Overview

    Instead of interpolating between data points in a continuous embedding space, program synthesis searches for discrete programs, or models, that perfectly explain observed data allowing it to achieve much greater generalization power with extreme data-efficiency, requiring only a few examples to learn. Intuitively we can understand that deep learning is majorly about continuous optimization while program synthesis is more of discrete search. Both ultimately seek a program that maps inputs to outputs. The difference lies in how they search for it. Deep learning starts with a parameterized function and continuously adjusts billions of weights using gradient descent until the observed error is minimized. Program synthesis instead searches directly through a space of executable programs, attempting to find one that exactly satisfies a specification.
    Historically, the field began with formal logic and correctness guarantees, long before neural networks entered the picture. Over time, however, the focus shifted from proving programs correct to generating useful programs efficiently, eventually leading to today’s neural and hybrid approaches. The earliest synthesis systems lived in a world that many deep learning practitioners would find unfamiliar: a world with no datasets, no training loops, and no generalization error. In deductive synthesis, the user provides a precise formal specification describing the desired behaviour. The synthesizer then applies logical inference rules to derive a program that is mathematically guaranteed to satisfy that specification. This idea is rooted in the famous “proofs-as-programs” correspondence, where constructing a proof effectively constructs a correct program. In some sense, deductive synthesis represents the ultimate zero-error regime: correctness is not measured empirically but established formally. Unfortunately, real-world users rarely provide mathematical specifications. They provide examples. This led to the emergence of Inductive Program Synthesis (IPS), often called Programming by Example. Imagine being given only a handful of input-output pairs and being asked to infer the underlying procedure. Unlike supervised learning, which typically relies on thousands or millions of examples and learns a statistical approximation, inductive synthesis searches for a program that exactly fits every observed example. The objective is not to minimize a loss function but to satisfy a set of constraints perfectly. Techniques such as enumerative search, SAT solving, and SMT solving became central because the search space is discrete rather than differentiable. As synthesis problems became larger and more complex, researchers began looking for ways to inject prior knowledge into the search process. This led to schema-guided synthesis and Sketching. The key idea is remarkably similar to architectural inductive bias in deep learning. A convolutional network already assumes locality and translation invariance before seeing any data. Likewise, a sketch provides a partial program structure and leaves only certain pieces unspecified. Instead of searching over all possible programs, the synthesizer only fills the holes. By injecting domain knowledge directly into the search process, sketching dramatically reduces combinatorial complexity and often turns impossible synthesis problems into tractable ones. Then came large language models. Instead of formal specifications or carefully crafted examples, users could simply describe intent in natural language. Modern code models generate programs by predicting tokens, much like they generate essays or conversations. At their core, these systems synthesize programs through probabilistic next-token prediction, generating code one token at a time conditioned on a natural language specification and their learned prior over source code. This dramatically improved usability and scale. Suddenly, synthesizing a database query or a web application became a prompting problem rather than a theorem-proving problem. However, something important was lost in the transition. Traditional synthesis systems could provide correctness guarantees. LLMs provide probabilities. A generated program may look convincing while still being subtly incorrect. This tension has pushed the field toward neuro-symbolic synthesis. Rather than choosing between neural networks and symbolic reasoning, hybrid systems attempt to combine them. The neural component acts as a powerful prior, rapidly proposing candidate programs or promising search directions. The symbolic component acts as a verifier, checking constraints, executing programs, and pruning invalid solutions. One can think of the neural network as intuition and the symbolic engine as rigor. The hope is to obtain the scalability and flexibility of deep learning while retaining the reliability of formal reasoning. Viewed historically, program synthesis has evolved from purely logical systems, to inductive search over examples, to schema-guided and sketch-based approaches, to neural code generators, and finally toward sophisticated neuro-symbolic hybrids.
    Interestingly, modern benchmarks such as ARC have revived renewed interest in many of these ideas. ARC tasks provide only a handful of examples and require discovering a transformation that generalizes beyond them, a setting that closely resembles the core problem of inductive synthesis. Yet pure symbolic approaches struggle with combinatorial search, while purely neural approaches often struggle with systematic generalization and verification. As a result, there is growing interest in neuro-symbolic systems that combine neural pattern recognition with symbolic search and formal reasoning. Whether the future lies in large language models, program synthesis, or some deeper synthesis of the two remains an open question. What is clear, however, is that many of the challenges exposed by ARC are pushing the field back toward a question that has existed since the earliest days of AI: can intelligence be built by learning patterns alone, or must it also discover explicit procedures that explain them?

    A deeper dive into the nuances of traditional program synthesis

    An elegant example of traditional program synthesis can be found in a tool most of us have used countless times: Excel’s FlashFill. Given just a few examples of how you would like data to be transformed, FlashFill can often infer the desired operation and automatically complete the remaining entries. Surprisingly, this feature was developed long before the current wave of deep learning-powered systems and does not rely on neural networks, gradient descent, or large-scale training data. Before diving into how FlashFill works, it is worth understanding the three major ideas that shaped traditional program synthesis: deductive synthesis, inductive synthesis, and schema guided synthesis through sketches. The earliest systems were based on deductive synthesis. Instead of providing training data, the user specifies the desired behavior as a logical formula, typically of the form ∀x ∃z R(x, z), where x represents the inputs, z the outputs, and R(x, z) the relationship that must hold between them. The synthesizer then acts like a theorem prover, constructing a proof that such an output exists. The program is extracted directly from this proof, guaranteeing correctness by construction. For a deep learning practitioner, this is the ultimate zero generalization-error regime, though writing these formal specifications is often harder than writing the program itself. Researchers therefore moved toward inductive synthesis, often called Programming by Example. Rather than providing mathematical specifications, users provide a small number of input-output examples. At first glance this resembles few-shot learning, but the underlying philosophy is very different. A neural network statistically generalizes from examples; an inductive synthesizer performs a combinatorial search through a space of candidate programs until it finds one that exactly satisfies every example. There is no notion of minimizing a loss function. The candidate program is either correct or incorrect. As synthesis tasks became more complex, search quickly became intractable. To manage this, researchers introduced schemas and sketches. A sketch is a partially specified program containing “holes” that the synthesizer must fill. This idea is remarkably similar to architectural inductive bias in deep learning. Just as a CNN restricts the hypothesis space by assuming locality and translation invariance, a sketch restricts the program space by fixing part of the structure beforehand. Instead of searching for every possible program, the synthesizer only searches for what belongs inside the holes. With this background, we can finally understand FlashFill. Consider the following task: John Doe → J. Doe Alice Cooper → A. Cooper and suppose we want to transform: Jane Smith → ? A deep learning practitioner would likely frame this as a sequence-to-sequence translation problem. We could collect thousands of examples, tokenize the strings, train a Transformer using cross-entropy loss, and learn a probabilistic mapping from names to formatted outputs. FlashFill takes a radically different approach. FlashFill begins with the assumption that the user’s intent can be expressed as a small program written in a carefully designed Domain Specific Language (DSL). This language contains operations such as: • Extract substring • Locate delimiters • Select tokens • Concatenate strings • Insert constants The goal is not to learn weights. The goal is to discover a program. For example, FlashFill might hypothesize: Take first character of first token + “. " + second token which would produce: John Doe → J. Doe Alice Cooper → A. Cooper Jane Smith → J. Smith Notice what has happened. FlashFill has transformed a data problem into a search problem. Unfortunately, many different programs may explain the same examples. Given only: John Doe → J. Doe all of the following programs are valid: • Take first character + second token • Take everything before first space and shorten it • Take position 1 and append text after position 5 • … The challenge is therefore not merely finding a program. The challenge is finding the program that best captures the user’s intent. Naively, one might imagine enumerating every possible program in the DSL and testing them one by one. This quickly becomes impossible. Even a small collection of string manipulation operators can generate millions of candidate programs. The key innovation behind FlashFill is Version Space Algebra (VSA). Rather than representing candidate programs individually, FlashFill represents entire families of programs simultaneously using a compact graph structure. When the first example is provided, FlashFill constructs a graph containing every program capable of producing the desired output. When a second example is provided, another graph is constructed. The synthesis process then becomes one of intersecting these graphs, eliminating enormous numbers of invalid programs in a single operation. A useful way to think about VSA is not as search, but as elimination. Instead of asking: “Which program should I try next?” FlashFill asks: “Which entire classes of programs can I eliminate immediately?” Every additional example collapses the version space further until only a small set of plausible programs remains. At this point FlashFill faces one final problem. Multiple programs may still satisfy every example. The system therefore ranks candidate programs according to carefully designed heuristics. Simpler transformations are preferred over highly specific ones. Programs that appear more generalizable are ranked higher than programs that merely memorize the examples. In many ways, this ranking stage is where FlashFill attempts to infer the user’s true intent. FlashFill highlights the fundamental trade-off between traditional program synthesis and deep learning. Neural networks operate over smooth, differentiable landscapes and excel when data is plentiful and noisy. Program synthesis operates over discrete program spaces and excels when examples are scarce but correctness matters. One side offers statistical generalization; the other offers exact reasoning. Interestingly, the setup of FlashFill (discovering a latent procedure from only a handful of examples) looks strikingly similar to the challenge of settings encountered in ARC-style reasoning tasks. This is one reason modern neuro-symbolic research has revived many ideas from traditional program synthesis, combining neural pattern recognition with symbolic search and verification.

    Modern Approaches - Neuro-Symbolic Program Synthesis

    As deep learning systems became increasingly successful, researchers naturally began asking whether the strengths of neural networks and symbolic reasoning could be combined. A useful overview of these integration strategies is provided by Henry Kautz’s neuro-symbolic taxonomy, which categorizes the many ways learning and reasoning can interact. For our purposes, however, the exact categories are less important than the key ideas they introduced. One of the earliest insights was that symbolic knowledge need not remain separate from neural networks. Logical constraints, domain knowledge, and expert rules can be injected directly into the training process, influencing how representations are learned. Symbolic structure acts as an additional inductive bias, guiding optimization toward more meaningful solutions. A second line of research explored the opposite direction: using neural networks to assist symbolic reasoning. Traditional program synthesis systems often struggled because the search space of possible programs grows combinatorially. Neural models can learn useful priors over this search space, predicting which hypotheses, programs, or reasoning steps are most promising.
    Researchers also explored incorporating symbolic constraints directly into neural objectives. Rather than treating logical rules as hard requirements, they can be expressed as differentiable constraints or regularizers that influence the loss function itself. In this view, symbolic reasoning becomes another source of supervision, encouraging models to learn representations that remain consistent with known structure. Another important direction involves combining neural perception with symbolic reasoning. Neural networks excel at transforming raw sensory inputs such as images, audio, and language into useful representations, while symbolic systems excel at operating on structured objects and relations. Hybrid architectures therefore often use neural components for perception and abstraction while relying on symbolic components for planning, reasoning, verification, or search. More recently, large language models have accelerated interest in neuro-symbolic systems even further. Modern reasoning architectures increasingly combine neural generation with symbolic verification, execution engines, theorem provers, interpreters, planners, or search procedures. Neural models generate candidate solutions, while symbolic components validate, refine, or reject them.
    Although these paradigms differ significantly in implementation, they all represent attempts to bridge the same gap. The central challenge is determining how these two forms of computation should cooperate. For different purposes different solutions were needed to be administered.
    Benchmarks like ARC open our eyes to the fact that despite decades of progress, no existing paradigm has yet demonstrated the level of abstraction, adaptability, and sample efficiency displayed by humans. This naturally leads us to the next question: if we already know how to combine learning and reasoning, what is still missing?

    Towards AGI – A Vision to Combine the Two Paradigms

    If the past decade of research has taught us anything, it is that neither pure deep learning nor pure symbolic reasoning appears sufficient on its own. Deep learning excels at extracting patterns from vast amounts of data, while symbolic systems excel at manipulating explicit abstractions and enforcing structure. Neuro-symbolic systems have made significant progress by combining these strengths, yet a fundamental gap remains. Benchmarks such as ARC make this limitation particularly visible. Unlike most machine learning tasks, ARC provides only a handful of examples and requires discovering a transformation that generalizes beyond them. The challenge is not merely recognizing patterns, searching through candidate programs, or recalling previously learned solutions. Instead, it often requires constructing entirely new abstractions from minimal evidence. Humans perform this routinely. Modern AI systems still struggle. This observation points toward a deeper question. Perhaps intelligence is not simply the ability to learn functions, nor merely the ability to search over symbolic structures. Perhaps it is the ability to rapidly acquire new skills, construct useful abstractions, and reuse them across entirely novel situations. In other words, the challenge may not be reasoning itself, but learning how to reason efficiently in previously unseen environments. This perspective has motivated a growing number of research efforts. Projects such as DreamCoder explored the idea of continually learning new abstractions while solving tasks, effectively compressing previous experience into reusable building blocks. More recently, organizations such as Symbolica and NDEA have argued that future intelligent systems may require richer mechanisms for abstraction formation, compositional reasoning, world modelling, and program-like representations that go beyond both traditional symbolic systems and today’s large language models. A recurring theme across these efforts is the notion of skill acquisition efficiency—the ability to learn a new capability from very little experience. Human children do not require thousands of examples to understand concepts such as symmetry, containment, counting, or object permanence. They appear to possess a collection of priors that allow them to rapidly construct explanations for unfamiliar situations. Understanding how such abstractions are represented, acquired, and reused remains one of the central open questions in AI. Viewed through this lens, ARC is less a benchmark and more a diagnostic tool. It reveals that current systems can often recognize patterns, retrieve knowledge, and even perform sophisticated reasoning, yet still struggle to form entirely new abstractions from sparse evidence. The challenge is not simply more parameters, more data, or more compute. It is understanding how intelligent systems can efficiently construct the concepts needed to solve problems they have never encountered before. Whether the path forward ultimately resembles program synthesis, large language models, neuro-symbolic systems, or an entirely new paradigm remains unknown. What is becoming increasingly clear, however, is that intelligence appears to require both powerful pattern recognition and explicit reasoning, combined with an ability that neither paradigm has fully captured: the rapid formation of new abstractions. The search for that missing ingredient remains one of the most important challenges on the road toward Artificial General Intelligence.
